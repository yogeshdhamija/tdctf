### Game Design Document: Multiplayer Tower Defense Capture the Flag

#### `1. Game Modes`

* **Multiplayer (1v1):** The primary, core experience of the game. A competitive, grid-based, player vs. player match lasting around 20 minutes per game.  
* **Single-Player Campaign:** A narrative-driven comprehensive, escalating tutorial. It introduces mechanics, unit types, and the pacing of the game organically within a story context, preparing players for multiplayer without overwhelming them upfront.  
* **Vs. Bots:** An offline or low-stakes environment for practice, testing build orders, and casual play against a simple AI opponent.

#### `2. Core Concept`

A competitive, grid-based multiplayer Tower Defense (TD) with a Capture the Flag (CTF) objective. Two players compete on a shared map to penetrate enemy defenses, steal their flag, and return it to a designated receptacle. Players create their own defenses (towers) and invest in their attacking army (creeps).

The core gameplay loop centers around planning, anticipating the enemy, discovery, and observation: you build your defenses and offenses by anticipating your enemy's next moves, and then watch the simulated outcome of those decisions play out. This simulation also has a discovery element: revealing new information about your enemy.

The fun part is seeing if the simulation will play out as you expect with unexpected novelty and obstacles generated by the enemy.

#### `3. Game Pacing & Flow`

The game has turns, but both players perform their turn at the same time. Each turn starts with the planning phase, where players choose to build & upgrade, and ends with the action phase, where the players can see the simulated result.

* **Planning Phase:** Players spend resources to place towers, upgrade existing towers, and progress their attacking creep army. Resources are spent immediately, but construction and upgrades may take multiple turns to complete.  
  * The planning phase ends when both players click “Lock In”.  
  * Or, the planning phase ends when the timer expires (60s, but configurable before the game starts at the same place the map is chosen for the game).  
* **Action Phase:** Creeps spawn and execute their pathing. Players do not intervene during this phase; they watch the outcome of their investments and try to glean what the opponent is working towards.  
  * The action phase ends when all creeps die, or if the game ends.

#### `4. Resources, Balance, and Escalation`

Players manage a single, unified currency:

* **Resources:** The sole economic driver of the game. Resources are earned at a fixed rate per turn and through special towers and creeps. This single pool funds tower construction, tower upgrades, and the Creep Skill Tree.  
  * **Resource generating tower**: an expensive tower that takes a long time to build but generates resources every turn after that.  
  * **Resource retrieving creeps**: an early creep skill tree upgrade that adds retriever creeps which, instead of the flag, retrieve damaged enemy tower debris into the home receptacle which generates resources.  
* **The Escalation Cap:** A physical map acts as a natural timer. Tower real estate is limited on the grid meaning defensive power eventually hits a hard cap. Conversely, investments in the Creep Skill Tree can scale much higher, guaranteeing that defenses will eventually break, preventing endless stalemates.

#### `5. The Flag`

The flag is a dynamic piece of the map's ecosystem.

* **Aura Buff:** The flag emits a radius that buffs the defenses of the player who owns it.  
* **Dropped Flags & Rubber-Banding:** If an enemy creep steals the flag but dies on the return journey, the flag stays exactly where it dropped. It continues to buff the defending player's towers in that new location. This creates a catch-up mechanic: failed attacks effectively deliver a defensive buff directly into the defender's kill-zones, which changes the dynamic and makes subsequent theft attempts more difficult in that spot (so the same strategy might not work twice). However, it also rewards the attacker by invalidating all the towers further ahead which are now useless.

#### `6. Fog of War`

Information about the enemy is limited. A player's vision only extends in a radius around their active creeps and towers: anything outside that radius is frozen in time to what the creeps last saw. If your creeps have never gotten to an area, it only shows the empty map (therefore starting flag/receptacle locations and default creep pathing is always visible). The enemy can see your tower placement only if their creeps or towers made it far enough to see them; once their creeps die or move on, their vision of your towers is frozen to that point in time. 

During the build phase, you can see and inspect the corpses of the last round of creeps. You can see that an enemy is building/upgrading a tower by seeing the construction at that location, but you cannot see which tower/upgrade it is. More information may be available through specialized creep upgrades (e.g., dedicated scouting units which can determine towers and upgrades in progress).

#### `7. Tech Trees: Creeps vs. Towers`

The game utilizes two distinct avenues of technological progression:

* **Creep Skill Tree (Global):** A tree where the player makes choices which move them towards future options. Once the cost is paid, the research may take multiple turns to complete and “turn on”. Upgrades provide global buffs and spawn specialized creeps each turn. The contents of the research queue are hidden from the enemy, but players can inspect the attacking creeps (and their corpses from the last turn) to see what research was already finished by the last turn.  
* **Tower Upgrade Tree (Local):** Individual to each tower. After the cost is paid, the tower may take multiple turns to upgrade, during which time it acts at its previous capacity. Upgrades provide localized damage and utility.

#### `8. The Creep Ecosystem`

Each creep upgrade either spawns additional creeps each turn, or enhances existing creeps. To maintain visual legibility, unit counts are kept deliberately small. A player's wave will begin with just 1 retriever creep and scale to a usual maximum of roughly 50 creeps in the late game. Examples of creep variations include:

* **Retriever Creeps:** The default units that attempt to run the maze, soak tower damage, and steal the flag.   
* **Siege Creeps:** Units that attack enemy towers, damaging them and eventually destroying them.  
* **Scout Creeps:** Specialized recon units that expand vision, and pierce the Fog of War to reveal details about enemy tower types and active research deeper in the maze. 

Example of a creep upgrade path:

1. Spawn a scout creep per turn with 2x the vision radius of a normal creep.  
2. Enhance scout creeps to see enemy tower upgrades in progress within their vision radius.  
3. Enhance scout creeps to further provide vision for 2 turns after their death around their corpse.

Another example:

1. Spawn two siege creeps which melee attacks the first enemy tower they encounter.  
2. Enhance the siege creeps to have more damage.  
3. Double the number of melee siege creeps.  
   

#### `9. The Tower Ecosystem`

There is a variety (\~10) of base towers, each of which is unique and may interact with anything in the game (example: a tower which interacts with and changes the map by laying down creep-damaging spikes, a tower which interacts with enemy towers by “stealing energy” from all enemy electric towers, etc.)

Each tower has a small upgrade tree unique to that tower (three or four levels), and investment in the upgrade only upgrades that individual tower. The upgrade tree may have choices– making one choice lock the tower out of the other path.

Towers may be destroyed during the planning phase, but the cost is not refunded.

#### `10. Map Dynamics & Mazing`

The game takes place on grid-based maps. Creep pathing follows a demarcated line visible to both players through the map and players may obstruct this path with their towers placed on the grid. The creep pathing algorithm is: “take the shortest unblocked path to any point on the line that’s ahead of the furthest point you’ve already visited”

- Note: this may mean that creeps “know” information that hasn’t been revealed to the player through the fog of war. (Example: at a fork in the opponent’s maze, the creeps know that one path doesn’t outlet to the flag but the other does)

##### Map Variety:

Maps introduce variety through unique layouts, varying spawn points, and different flag receptacle locations. There are specific build zones:

* Neutral: Contested parts of the grid where both players can build towers and compete for real estate. If both players claim the same location, mutual destruction occurs at the beginning of the simulation resulting in path-blocking but non-functional debris.   
* Red/blue-only: The majority in most maps: the traditional tower defense experience where each player is free to build their towers to obstruct and attack the attacking creeps.
