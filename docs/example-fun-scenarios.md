# Example Scenarios that Demonstrate the Fun
The following scenarios illustrate the kind of emergent, cascading decision-making that emerges from the intersection of the game's systems. They are not exhaustive — they are meant to communicate the *texture* of strategic depth the design aims to enable.

##### Scenario A: Cascading Choices on the Creep Skill Tree
A player invests early in **scout creeps**, sacrificing some damage-soaking and damage-dealing capability in exchange for vision. This unlocks a path toward **undead creeps**, whose corpses continue to provide vision into the next build phase — granting persistent reconnaissance deep into enemy territory.

Armed with this vision, the player can now confidently commit to **ranged siege creeps** rather than melee, knowing that the damage dealing towers currently present are better against melee. From here, the path branches again:
- Invest in **shield assault creeps** that force towers to target them, soaking damage while the siege creeps work safely behind them, *or*
- Invest in a **range upgrade** for siege creeps, allowing them to outrange specific tower archetypes entirely.

Each early choice doesn't just add power — it shapes the set of meaningful decisions available later. A wrong early commitment narrows the late-game tree; a right one opens devastating combos.

##### Scenario B: Cascading Choices on the Defense Side
A player invests in a **large quantity of cheap blocker towers** to reshape enemy pathing. The blockers create a deliberate blind spot: a region the enemy's current scouting tier cannot see into. Hidden in that blind spot, the player begins constructing a **sniper tower** — initially weak, but progressively upgraded over several turns.

By the time the enemy notices their heavy creeps dying faster than expected, the sniper tower is already capable of one-shotting them. The opponent now faces a difficult choice: invest in scouting (delaying their offensive push), push harder hoping to overwhelm before the sniper scales further, or change flavors of creeps which aren't as vulnerable.

The depth here is that a single cheap-tower decision early on wasn't really about damage — it was about *manufacturing fog of war* on a map that nominally offers none.

##### Scenario C: Information Warfare and Misdirection
A player observes through their scouts that the enemy has not invested in any tanky creep upgrades. Confident their lightweight wave-clearing tower can handle anything coming, the player builds that tower **visibly near the front** — a bluff of their strategy.

Meanwhile, deeper in their territory and outside enemy vision, the player builds a **completely different tower type**. The enemy, only seeing the front-line build, invests in creep upgrades designed to counter it — wasting resources on a counter to a tower that isn't even the real threat. When their creeps finally reach the hidden tower, they're poorly equipped to deal with it.

The fun here lives in the asymmetry of information: the player isn't just hiding their build — they're using their *visible* build as a piece of intentional misdirection.

##### Scenario D: The Flag as Bait and Punishment
The player guesses that the opponent is researching an additional retriever creep, which would be enough to break through their killbox. The player intentionally forgoes building additional power at the killbox. Instead, the player uses their turn to build a long switchback of towers that'll affect enemy pathing to be much longer. EXCEPT: they purposely leave a hole there.

Next turn, the opponent does indeed retrieve the flag but dies on the return journey close to the kill box. Now the flag is buffing the kill box. The player now closes the hole-- the flag is now actually further than its original position, and the killbox is buffed.

##### Scenario E: Mutually Assured Construction in Neutral Zones
A neutral build zone is positioned perfectly for an expensive **AoE tower** — both players know it would be a tremendous asset. Rather than commit the resources and risk mutual destruction, one player places a **cheap, near-useless tower** in that spot, betting that the opponent will commit their expensive AoE tower the same turn.

If the bet pays off: the opponent wastes a major investment, both towers are destroyed, and the resulting debris reshapes pathing — potentially in the bluffing player's favor. If the bet fails: the opponent built elsewhere, and the bluffer has merely wasted a small amount on a token tower.

This is poker with concrete: a small bet to potentially blow up a much larger one.

##### Scenario F: The Offense-Defense Economic Tension
A player commits early to offense, hoping for a quick win. Their first wave of upgraded creeps isn't quite strong enough to overwhelm enemy defenses — but each creep killed feeds the opponent resources. The opponent now faces a critical read:
- If the aggressor is *still* investing in offense, the defender must keep pouring resources into defense or lose.
- If the aggressor has quietly *switched* to defense, the defender is wasting resources on defenses against an attack that isn't coming — falling behind on their own offensive tech.

The aggressor knows the defender can't see their current research. The defender must decide whether to call the bluff. Every turn spent guessing wrong is a turn the other player pulls ahead. This dynamic ensures that even an apparently "failed" offensive can be a deliberate strategic gambit rather than a loss.

---

What ties these scenarios together is the **layering of incomplete information, multi-turn commitment, and cascading consequence**. No decision is isolated: every tower placed, every creep researched, every flag attempt — successful or not — reshapes the strategic landscape for the rest of the match. The simulation phase isn't just a payoff; it's the reveal of how well each player read the other.
